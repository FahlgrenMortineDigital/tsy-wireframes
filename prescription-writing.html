Writing PN Prescriptions

Parenteral nutrition order writing is consistently identified as one of the biggest knowledge gaps for clinicians managing patients who require intravenous nutritional support. Accurately prescribing PN requires an understanding of macronutrient and micronutrient dosing, fluid balance, electrolyte management, and compatibility constraints — all tailored to the individual patient's clinical status and metabolic demands.

FDS: Content Block / Callout

Practice Gap

Many clinicians receive limited formal training on PN order writing during their medical, pharmacy, or nursing education. Surveys indicate that a significant proportion of healthcare professionals feel underprepared to independently write or evaluate PN prescriptions, leading to reliance on nutrition support teams or institutional templates without a full understanding of the underlying rationale.

Components of a PN Order

A systematic, step-by-step approach to PN order writing helps reduce errors and ensures all essential components are addressed. The following eight steps outline the standard workflow for composing a PN prescription.

1

Patient Assessment

Obtain and document current weight, height, BMI, and nutritional status. Review relevant labs, fluid balance, and organ function to inform dosing decisions.

2

Calculate Energy Requirements

Determine total caloric goals using kcal/kg/day based on patient population and clinical condition. Consider indirect calorimetry when available for critically ill patients.

3

Determine Protein Requirements

Set amino acid goals in g/kg/day based on clinical status, degree of catabolism, renal function, and hepatic function. Protein needs are often higher in critically ill and surgical patients.

4

Set Dextrose Concentration and Rate

Select the appropriate dextrose concentration and calculate the glucose infusion rate (GIR). Monitor to prevent hyperglycemia, keeping GIR below the maximum oxidation rate of 4–5 mg/kg/min in adults.

5

Select Lipid Emulsion Type and Dose

Choose the appropriate lipid emulsion based on clinical context. Dose lipids in g/kg/day, typically not exceeding 1 g/kg/day in adults. Consider composite lipid emulsions containing fish oil, olive oil, or MCT for specific patient populations.

6

Add Electrolytes Based on Labs

Include sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, phosphate, chloride, and acetate based on current serum levels, renal function, and ongoing losses. Adjust daily based on monitoring results.

7

Include Vitamins and Trace Elements

Add a daily multivitamin preparation and a multi-trace element additive to all PN formulations. Consider additional supplementation of specific micronutrients (e.g., thiamine, zinc, selenium) based on clinical need.

8

Specify Fluid Volume and Infusion Rate

Calculate total fluid volume considering all IV fluids, medications, and PN components. Set the infusion rate and duration (continuous vs. cyclic) based on patient tolerance and clinical goals.

Common Prescribing Errors

PN prescribing errors remain a significant source of preventable patient harm. Understanding the most frequent error categories helps clinicians avoid common pitfalls and improve prescription accuracy.

FDS: Feature Card

Overfeeding

Providing excess calories — particularly from dextrose — can lead to hyperglycemia, hepatic steatosis, increased CO2 production, and difficulty weaning from mechanical ventilation. Overfeeding is one of the most common and clinically significant PN prescribing errors.

FDS: Feature Card

Underfeeding

Inadequate caloric or protein provision leads to continued malnutrition, impaired wound healing, loss of lean body mass, and increased risk of infection. Underfeeding may result from overly conservative dosing or failure to advance PN to goal.

FDS: Feature Card

Electrolyte Errors

Incorrect electrolyte dosing — including omission of key electrolytes, failure to adjust for renal function, or exceeding calcium-phosphate solubility limits — can cause serious metabolic derangements and precipitation within the PN solution.

FDS: Feature Card

Lipid Dosing Errors

Dosing lipid emulsions at historical rates (e.g., using outdated soybean-oil-only protocols) instead of current evidence-based guidelines can lead to excessive omega-6 fatty acid exposure, hepatic complications, and suboptimal clinical outcomes.

FDS: Content Block / Callout

The Shift to Standardized PN Orders

Healthcare institutions are increasingly adopting computerized provider order entry (CPOE) systems with built-in PN order sets to replace handwritten prescriptions. Standardized electronic order forms reduce transcription errors, enforce dosing limits, flag compatibility issues, and integrate clinical decision support — significantly improving both the safety and efficiency of PN prescribing. ASPEN guidelines recommend the use of standardized PN order forms as a best practice for all institutions providing parenteral nutrition.
